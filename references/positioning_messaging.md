## Category strategy decision tree

### First question: enter an existing category or create a new one?

Use **category entry** when:

- Buyers already search for the category.
- Budget owners already exist.
- Competitors have pricing pages and review sites.
- The product's advantage can be explained as better, faster, cheaper, safer, easier, or more integrated.
- The team has limited budget and needs near-term revenue.

Use **category creation** only when:

- Existing categories actively mislead buyers about the product's value.
- The problem is real but not well named.
- Buyers compare you to the wrong alternatives.
- You can afford education, proof-building, narrative, community, and longer sales cycles.
- You have a clear "old way vs new way" story and repeated buyer language proving the new framing resonates.

Default for small teams: **enter a category to capture demand, then create a subcategory once proof accumulates.**

### Positioning by category mode

| Mode | Primary message | Best proof | Biggest risk | Best tests |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Category entry | "A better way to do known thing" | Feature comparison, customer outcomes, migration ease | Commodity pricing | Competitor landing tests, pricing page comparison, review mining |
| Subcategory | "Known category, new wedge" | Side-by-side workflow, expert proof, niche case studies | Too narrow or confusing | Niche outbound, use-case page, benchmark report |
| Category creation | "The old frame is broken; here is the new frame" | Thought leadership, market map, first-principles demo, customer stories | Long education cycle | Manifesto page, webinar, founder-led content, problem interviews |

## Positioning system

### Positioning statement

```text
[Product] is a [category] for [ICP] who need to [job/outcome].
Unlike [main alternative], it [differentiated mechanism] so customers can [measurable result] without [major pain/risk].
```

### Messaging hierarchy

| Layer | Question answered | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Category | "What is this?" | Known noun or deliberately named new frame |
| ICP | "Is it for me?" | Role, industry, workflow, company stage |
| Trigger | "Why now?" | Deadline, event, pain spike, change |
| Outcome | "Why care?" | Metric, money, time, risk, status |
| Mechanism | "Why will this work?" | Differentiated method, workflow, model, data, network |
| Proof | "Can I trust it?" | Case, benchmark, testimonial, security, demo, credentials |
| Offer | "What do I get?" | Package, price, guarantee, support, onboarding |
| CTA | "What should I do next?" | Trial, demo, audit, checklist, quote, pilot |

### Positioning stress tests

A positioning option fails if:

- The buyer cannot identify themselves in the first screen.
- The category noun causes the wrong comparison set.
- The differentiation is a feature, not an outcome or mechanism.
- The proof does not match the risk of purchase.
- The price implies an ICP different from the copy.
- The CTA asks for too much commitment before trust is earned.

## Trust and proof strategy

Match proof to buyer risk. The higher the risk, the more proof must be visible before the CTA.

| Risk level | Buyer risk | Proof required |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Free tool, newsletter, toy app | Clear screenshots, sample output, maker credibility |
| Medium | Paid self-serve, team workflow | Testimonials, refund policy, pricing clarity, docs, security basics |
| High | Business-critical SaaS, data, compliance | Case studies, references, security page, SLA, procurement docs, ROI proof |
| Very high | Regulated, health, finance, legal, enterprise | Substantiation, legal review, certifications, audit trail, explicit limitations |

Proof inventory:

- **Product proof:** demo, screenshots, sample outputs, benchmarks, sandbox, changelog.
- **Customer proof:** logos, testimonials, case studies, before/after, review quotes.
- **Operational proof:** uptime, security, privacy, support responsiveness, docs.
- **Expert proof:** credentials, advisors, integrations, certifications, analyst mentions.
- **Economic proof:** ROI calculator, time saved, error reduction, revenue gained, payback period.
- **Social proof:** communities, usage counts, public customer stories. Never fake it.

Proof rule: every strong claim needs matching evidence. If proof is weak, weaken the claim or turn it into a testable promise.

## Conversion copy system

### Copy rewrite pipeline

1. Identify ICP, trigger, job, alternative, and desired outcome.
2. Mine buyer language from interviews, reviews, communities, support tickets, sales calls.
3. Map objections and proof requirements.
4. Choose category and positioning statement.
5. Write message hierarchy.
6. Draft copy with one primary CTA.
7. Remove unsupported claims and vague adjectives.
8. Add proof where risk appears.
9. Test comprehension with 5-second and first-click tests.
10. Instrument CTA, form, demo, activation, and qualification events.

### Landing page anatomy

| Section | Job | Checklist |
|---|---|---|
| Hero | Relevance and promise | Category noun, ICP, outcome, mechanism, CTA |
| Problem | Cost of status quo | Specific pain, trigger, consequences, buyer language |
| Outcome | Future state | Metrics, workflow result, emotional relief/status |
| Mechanism | Why it works | 3–5 steps, differentiated method, screenshots |
| Proof | Trust | Case, testimonial, benchmark, logos, demo, security |
| Use cases | Segmentation | "For X / Y / Z" with distinct pains |
| Pricing/offer | Commitment | Packages, inclusions, guarantees, FAQ |
| Objections | Anxiety reduction | Migration, data, support, comparison, ROI |
| CTA | Next step | One primary action, low-friction secondary action |

### Hero formula bank

```text
[Outcome] for [ICP] without [pain/risk].
```

```text
The [category] built for [specific workflow/person].
```

```text
Replace [painful alternative] with [new mechanism] in [timeframe].
```

```text
Find/fix/ship [valuable thing] before [costly trigger].
```

```text
[Product] helps [ICP] [job] by [mechanism], so they can [measurable result].
```

### Copy quality checklist

- The first screen says what it is, who it is for, and why it matters.
- The buyer can recognize their current struggle.
- The page names the main alternative or status quo.
- Benefits are tied to measurable outcomes or concrete relief.
- Features explain the mechanism, not just inventory.
- Proof appears before high-friction CTAs.
- The CTA matches trust level.
- Pricing is clear or the reason for custom pricing is clear.
- Claims are substantiated or softened.
- No fake urgency, fake scarcity, fake reviews, bait-and-switch, or hidden terms.
