# Practice Digest: Field Rules for Empirical Marketing

## The 12 rules

1. The best segment is rarely the largest; it is the one with urgent pain, budget, reachability, proof, and low switching friction.
2. Category entry is usually cheaper than category creation. Enter where buyers already have language, then create a sharper subcategory once proof exists.
3. A buyer who says “interesting” has given almost no evidence. A buyer who pays, introduces a stakeholder, or shares data has given evidence.
4. Pricing is a segmentation tool. A low price may attract low-value customers and make high-touch support impossible.
5. The first proof asset should match the biggest buyer risk: demo for product risk, benchmark for performance risk, security page for data risk, case study for business risk.
6. Outbound is a segment test, not a spam engine. Bad outbound usually means bad list, trigger, offer, or message.
7. SEO/AEO only works if the market has language. If buyers do not search for the category, create problem and comparison content first.
8. A/B testing without sufficient traffic, correct assignment, and guardrails is theater. For small teams, run sharper qualitative and smoke tests first.
9. Activation is not account creation. Activation is first value.
10. Retention beats acquisition. If activated users do not return, more traffic multiplies waste.
11. Trust failures compound. Hidden pricing, vague claims, fake urgency, weak privacy, and hard cancellation all tax conversion.
12. A pivot is not failure when it protects the team from scaling a false positive.

8. Experiment table

## The qualified-intent mantra

A metric matters when it is:

- From a specific segment,
- Triggered by a real buyer context,
- Connected to the problem,
- Frictional enough to signal seriousness,
- Predictive of activation, revenue, retention, or referral.
